Binangonan, Rizal

A Scenic Gateway of the Highlands

Founded in 1900, Binangonan is a municipality that combines coastal and mountainous landscapes. It played a significant role during World War II as a site of resistance against Japanese forces. Angono-Binangonan Petroglyphs

These ancient rock carvings, shared with neighboring Angono, are considered the oldest known artworks in the Philippines, dating back to before 2000 BC. The 127 human and animal figures engraved on a rock wall provide a fascinating glimpse into the prehistoric culture of the region. The Angono-Binangonan Petroglyphs, located in Rizal province, are the oldest known artworks in the Philippines, believed to date back to approximately 3000 BCE. Discovered in 1965 by National Artist Carlos “Botong” Francisco during a field trip, the site features 127 distinct carvings etched into a volcanic rock wall. These carvings depict stylized human figures, animals such as frogs and lizards, and various geometric shapes. The petroglyphs are thought to have served ritualistic or healing purposes, providing a glimpse into the spiritual and cultural practices of prehistoric Filipinos. The site itself is a shallow rock shelter, measuring approximately 63 meters in width, 8 meters in depth, and 5 meters in height. It is formed in volcanic tuffs of the Quaternary period, likely created by faulting. Recognized for its historical significance, the Angono-Binangonan Petroglyphs were declared a National Cultural Treasure in 1973. Efforts have been made to preserve and protect the site from natural erosion and human-induced damage. Today, the petroglyphs stand as a testament to the rich cultural heritage of the Philippines and are a must-visit for those interested in archaeology and history.

Binalayan Festival

This vibrant festival celebrates Binangonan's rich culture, faith, and prosperity through street dances, colorful performances, and traditional rituals. It showcases the town's fishing heritage and the resilience of its people. Binalayan Festival is an annual cultural celebration held in Binangonan, Rizal, honoring the town's rich history, artistic heritage, and vibrant community spirit. Situated between the foothills of Sierra Madre and the shores of Laguna de Bay, Binangonan is Rizal province's oldest and first-class municipality, encompassing 72.70 square kilometers and 40 barangays, including 17 from Talim Island and 23 from the mainland. The festival's name, "Binalayan," is derived from the town's original name, "Binangonan," which means "place of the bell" in Tagalog. This name is linked to the town's historical significance and its association with the bell used in local religious practices.​ A highlight of the Binalayan Festival is the "Gewang-gewang," a traditional dance performed by locals while carrying the image of St. Ursula. This dance showcases the community's devotion and artistic expression, reflecting the town's deep-rooted cultural traditions. The Binalayan Festival not only celebrates Binangonan's rich cultural heritage but also fosters community pride and unity. It serves as a platform for showcasing local talent, preserving traditions, and promoting the town's unique identity to both residents and visitors.

Fishing Culture

Binangonan's lakeside communities maintain traditional boat-making and fishing practices. The town is particularly known for its inihaw na tilapia (grilled tilapia) and other freshwater fish dishes from Laguna de Bay, prepared using generations-old recipes. Local fishermen employ various traditional methods to harvest fish from the lake. One such technique is the use of the "bunbon", a traditional fish trap designed to catch fish effectively. These traps are strategically placed in the lake, taking advantage of the fish's natural movements and behaviors. The community's deep understanding of the lake's ecology and the behavior of different fish species has been passed down through generations, ensuring sustainable practices.​cDespite its abundance, Laguna de Bay faces environmental challenges that impact the fishing industry. Pollution and overfishing have led to a decline in fish populations, affecting the livelihoods of local fishermen. Conservation efforts are underway to address these issues, focusing on sustainable fishing practices, pollution control, and habitat restoration to ensure the long-term health of the lake and its surrounding communities.

Mt. Tagapo

Also known as "Susong Dalaga" for its breast-shaped peak, this mountain on Talim Island offers panoramic views of Laguna de Bay. The hike provides a unique perspective of Binangonan's natural beauty, combining lake and mountain landscapes. Mt. Tagapo, also known as Mt. Susong Dalaga, is the highest peak on Talim Island in Binangonan, Rizal, standing at 438 meters above sea level. It offers stunning panoramic views of Laguna de Bay, the Sierra Madre mountains, and even the distant skylines of Metro Manila. The mountain's name, "Susong Dalaga" (Maiden's Breast), comes from its conical shape, resembling the breast of a maiden, and is a popular landmark for hikers and nature enthusiasts. The hike to the summit is beginner-friendly with a difficulty level of 2/9, making it accessible to most hikers. The round-trip trail is approximately 6.5 kilometers long, with an estimated hiking time of 1.5 to 2 hours. The journey offers a blend of natural beauty, from walking through bamboo forests to climbing grassy slopes. At the summit, hikers are rewarded with breathtaking 360-degree views of Laguna de Bay, nearby mountains like Mt. Sembrano, and even Mt. Banahaw on clear days. To reach the mountain, visitors first travel to Binangonan Port from Metro Manila via jeepney or van. From there, a boat ride takes them to Barangay Janosa on Talim Island. Once on the island, hikers can register at the base camp and hire a local guide, which costs approximately ₱400 per group. The hike itself is easy to moderate, taking around 1.5 to 2 hours to reach the peak. For a safe and enjoyable hike, it is recommended to bring essentials such as sunscreen, a hat, enough water, snacks, and rain gear. The best time to hike Mt. Tagapo is during the dry season from November to April, as the weather can get hot during midday. The views from the summit, combined with the serene surroundings, make it a rewarding destination for nature lovers and adventure seekers.

Historical Content of Binangonan

Founded in 1900, Binangonan has developed a unique identity shaped by its geography - part lakeside community, part highland town. During World War II, it served as an important site of resistance against Japanese forces, with local guerrillas using the mountainous terrain to their advantage.

The town's economy revolves around fishing in Laguna de Bay and agriculture in its upland areas. Traditional crafts like bamboo and rattan weaving remain important cottage industries, preserving cultural heritage while providing livelihood.

Binangonan's location makes it a strategic gateway between the lowland urban centers and the mountainous regions of Rizal. This has influenced its development as both a fishing hub and a starting point for nature adventures in the highlands.

Binangonan is a town in the province of Rizal with a rich history that dates back to the pre-colonial period. The name "Binangonan" is believed to have originated from the word "binañgonan," meaning "the first town established around the lake," referring to the legend of how towns around Laguna de Bay were named. In 1574, Spanish missionaries arrived in the region and interacted with the indigenous Aeta people. By 1621, Binangonan was established as a parish by the Franciscan missionaries, and they chose Santa Ursula as the patron saint of the church. The Santa Ursula Parish Church, built between 1792 and 1800 and renovated in 1853, is a historic church located at the heart of the town. In 1900, Binangonan became part of the newly created Rizal province under Act No. 137 of the Philippine Commission. However, it was temporarily merged with the towns of Morong, Baras, and Cardona in 1903 under Act No. 942, before being restored as an independent town again in 1903 under Act No. 948, and Angono was later rejoined with Binangonan. The Angono-Binangonan Petroglyphs, located on the boundary between Binangonan and Angono, is an important archaeological site containing human and animal-shaped carvings that are estimated to be over 3,000 years old. This is the oldest known rock art in the Philippines and an essential part of Binangonan’s history. During World War II, on February 25, 1945, as the people of Binangonan celebrated their festival in honor of Santa Ursula, they were liberated from the Japanese by American soldiers. The residents held a parade and offered food to the soldiers, and in return, the Americans gave chocolates, candies, and cigarettes. Today, Binangonan is a first-class municipality with a population of over 300,000 people. It is well known for its fishing industry, with the Binangonan Fish Port and cement plants like Rizal Cement and Grandspan being major economic drivers. The primary livelihood of the residents is fishing and farming. The history of Binangonan is a testament to the resilience and cultural richness of the town, from ancient times to the present.
